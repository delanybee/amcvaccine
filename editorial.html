A Methane AMC Case Study · April 2026

The fastest lever
we’re not pulling

Livestock methane is one of the most tractable climate problems alive. An Advance Market Commitment can unlock it, and the economics hold. For every $1 of commitment, the model returns $14 in avoided climate damage. Here is the case, end to end.

Delany Broome · UC Berkeley, Master of Climate Solutions 2026
In collaboration with Spark Climate Solutions · April 2026
Part I · The urgency

Cattle are responsible for a $10 billion annual climate bill

Methane accounts for roughly 30% of all global warming since pre-industrial times. Unlike CO₂, which accumulates for centuries, methane cycles out of the atmosphere in about 12 years, which means cutting it delivers measurable temperature relief within a decade. Scientists recognize it as the #1 fastest lever to slow near-term warming: the scale and speed of its mitigation impact are matched by the availability of mitigation measures already in development.

The source? About half of all human-caused methane comes from agriculture. Within agriculture, cattle dominate: enteric fermentation from the rumen generates 75% of all livestock methane. A single cow emits between 154 and 264 pounds of methane annually, roughly $100 in annual climate damage per animal.

The U.S. cattle herd (about 90 million animals) emitted 6.64 million tons of methane in 2022. At a social cost of $1,600 per ton, that is more than $10 billion in annual climate damage from a single, concentrated source. Methane’s short half-life means every year of delay is warming that cannot be retroactively avoided.

The technology to address this exists, or is close. Methane-reducing vaccines, feed additives, and genetic selection tools are all in development or early deployment. The problem is not scientific feasibility. The problem is economics.

Part II · The market failure

The deadlock nobody solves first

There is a coordination trap at the center of this problem. Buyers of low-methane outcomes (food companies, supply chains, and climate-focused brands) won’t commit to purchasing a product that doesn’t exist yet at commercial scale. Innovators won’t invest in scaling a product with no guaranteed buyers. Both positions are individually rational. Together they produce nothing.

For a firm considering R&D investment, the range of outcomes without any demand guarantee includes deep losses on the downside. Risk-aversion kills viable projects even when expected value is positive. Capital waits. The market fails before it starts.

“Buyers won’t purchase what doesn’t exist yet.
Builders won’t ship what nobody’s buying.
The market fails before it starts.”

Probability distribution of R&D project payoff under two scenarios. Without a demand guarantee, the left tail is wide and investment-suppressing.

Without an AMC, a rational firm assigns real probability to deep losses. The AMC truncates that left tail, installing a floor that makes the investment decision viable.

The AMC literature, grounded in the Pneumococcal precedent designed by Nobel Laureate Michael Kremer, shows that this mechanism works. When the demand guarantee is credible, suppliers price R&D risk accordingly and investment moves earlier. The feedback loop from deadlock to deployment can be broken by a single commitment.

Part III · The mechanism

An Advance Market Commitment installs a floor

An Advance Market Commitment works by converting a future demand signal into a present economic reality. Sponsors (governments, food companies, and philanthropies) commit in advance to purchase a defined volume of low-methane outcomes once a qualifying intervention reaches commercial scale. The critical design feature: sponsors pay nothing if no product succeeds. Zero downside for funders. Only upside for the atmosphere.

The Pneumococcal AMC helped deliver vaccines to 1.7 billion children in developing countries. The methane AMC draws on the same design principles and is co-developed with the same economist. This mechanism has a track record.

For livestock methane, the AMC is technology-agnostic by design: vaccines, genetics, feed additives, and slow-release boluses all qualify if they meet performance criteria. That flexibility lets innovation competition happen in parallel, accelerating time-to-market across multiple pathways simultaneously.

Chart 2 of 3 · The shift

The adoption frontier shifts right

Each curve shows the share of producers likely to adopt at a given net cost per animal-year. The AMC subsidy reduces farmer cost, expanding the addressable population at every price point.

The horizontal axis is farmer net cost per animal-year. Every dollar of AMC support moves producers leftward on the cost curve, turning non-adopters into adopters.

Adoption is the key multiplier. Every additional percentage point of U.S. cattle adoption represents roughly 900,000 animals and about $90 million in avoided annual climate damage. The model’s baseline assumption of 50% adoption is a midpoint estimate. The break-even rate is 3.5%: the minimum adoption where every dollar committed is justified.

Reading the studio

What is the benefit-cost ratio?

BCR is the total present value of social benefits divided by the total present value of social costs. A BCR of 14.41 means every dollar spent generates about $14.41 in avoided climate damages.

Most public infrastructure investments are considered strong at a BCR near 2-3. This ratio is high because methane abatement delivers large public benefits while intervention costs remain modest relative to herd scale.

The break-even adoption metric tells you the minimum adoption rate where BCR equals 1. Under baseline assumptions that is about 3.5%.

What counts as social cost?

Social cost here is the total resource cost society bears to make the intervention happen. It is not the social cost of methane itself.

It has two parts: AMC outlay for risk and adoption support, plus ongoing manufacturing and deployment after the AMC window closes.

Under annual dosing, the non-AMC component rises materially because more doses are manufactured and administered over each animal’s lifetime.

What do the sliders change?

Adoption and social value scale benefits upward. Dosing scenario is the biggest cost lever. Feasibility (eta) shifts R&D risk, which changes required AMC support.

Use these controls together to test where economics remain strong and where assumptions become fragile.

The most consequential open variable is dosing. Single-dose AMC outlay: $534 M. Annual dosing: $832 M. Prime + booster: $1.07 B. The BCR stays above 1 in all scenarios, but the coalition-building ask changes substantially. The conversation with a $534 M target reaches different funders than a $1 B ask.

The model is most robust to adoption rate variation. At 10% adoption (one-fifth of the baseline), the BCR under single-dose assumptions is still approximately 2.9. For comparison, many public infrastructure investments are considered strong at a BCR of 2.

Part V · The coalition

$750 million. COP30.
The window is open.

The current design target is a $750 million coalition commitment timed to announce at COP30 in Brazil, November 2025. Coordination is led by the Market Shaping Accelerator, Spark Climate Solutions, and the Global Methane Hub, with design input from Nobel Laureate Michael Kremer, CGD President Rachel Glennerster, and Professor Chris Snyder of Dartmouth’s Tuck School.

Three actor types make this coalition work. None can do it alone.

Corporates & Supply Chains

Offtake premiums and demand commitments that make the market real before the product ships. Food companies are the demand floor.

Philanthropic Capital

Early-stage catalytic funding that absorbs R&D risk and accelerates the validation timeline toward commercial scale.

Governments

R&D matching grants and public verification infrastructure that underpin market trust and no private actor can build alone.

About

Behind this work

This essay is the capstone work of an environmental scientist with eight years of experience in California state environmental work, now pursuing the Master of Climate Solutions at UC Berkeley’s Rausser College of Natural Resources.

The analysis stress-tests a single proposed mechanism, an Advance Market Commitment for a methane-reducing livestock vaccine, tested through four interconnected lenses: narrative, economic evidence, techno-economic modeling, and framework review. The full site has four sections; this page is the editorial distillation.

The work was produced in collaboration with Spark Climate Solutions, a nonprofit accelerating progress in underfunded, high-impact climate fields, and builds on Spark’s 2025 Livestock Enteric Methane Mitigation Roadmap.

Outside this work, Delany is active in the Berkeley Energy and Resources Collaborative, an enthusiastic gardener and cook, a student of the art of tarot, and a firm believer that climate work should leave room for joy.
