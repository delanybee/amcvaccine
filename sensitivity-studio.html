Reading the studio

What is the benefit-cost ratio?

BCR is the total present value of social benefits divided by the total present value of social costs. A BCR of 14.41 means every dollar spent generates about $14.41 in avoided climate damages.

For reference, most public infrastructure investments are considered strong at a BCR of 2 to 3. This ratio is so high because methane abatement is a pure public good with enormous positive externalities, the intervention cost is modest relative to the herd, and the social cost of methane ($1,600/ton) captures real damages that no individual producer has any financial reason to address on their own.

The break-even adoption metric tells you the minimum adoption rate where BCR equals exactly 1. Under baseline assumptions that is around 3.5%, meaning the investment pays for itself even if adoption disappoints dramatically.

What counts as social cost?

Social cost here is the total resource cost that society bears to make the intervention happen. It is not the same as the social cost of methane ($1,600/ton, representing climate damages). It is the cost of the solution.

It has two pieces. First, the AMC outlay ($534M at baseline): the commitment funders make to compensate firms for R&D risk and to subsidize adoption during a 10-year sales window. Second, the ongoing manufacturing and deployment costs ($263M at baseline) that society bears after the AMC window closes.

Under annual dosing, the non-AMC component jumps to $761M because far more doses are manufactured and administered over each animal's lifetime.

What do the sliders change?

Adoption rate scales benefits linearly. More animals vaccinated means more methane avoided, but costs stay the same, so BCR rises.

Social value also scales benefits linearly. Higher climate damage estimates per animal mean larger avoided damages for the same spend.

Dosing scenario is the biggest cost lever. Switching from baseline to annual dosing jumps the AMC from $534M to $832M and non-AMC costs from $263M to $761M, nearly doubling total costs while benefits stay the same. That roughly halves the BCR.

Feasibility (eta) adjusts risk. Lower scientific feasibility means firms need a larger risk premium to justify entering, which pushes AMC costs up and BCR down.

Key uncertainties flagged by reviewers: The vaccination regimen (single dose vs. annual vs. prime + booster) is the most consequential open parameter. Reviewers note that a single lifetime dose assumption may be unrealistic, and that annual or multi-dose regimens could push the AMC above $1 billion. The feasibility parameter eta = 0.90 may be optimistic given available data. Adoption dynamics, pricing logic, and global scaling assumptions are not yet fully incorporated.
