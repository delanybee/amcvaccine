Designing Innovation: Incentives for Methane Reduction

An Economic Design Analysis of an Advance Market Commitment for Methane Reduction

Chapter 1: Problem and Mechanism

Why methane, and why now

Enteric methane is produced by microbes in the rumen of cattle during digestion. It is a short-lived but highly potent greenhouse gas. Because methane breaks down in roughly a decade, reducing it can generate near-term climate benefits faster than many other interventions.

CHART FOCUS Value range per animal-year used in the economic model ($22, $30, $38).

Methane lifetime: approximately 10 years in the atmosphere.
Warming potential: around 80 times CO2 over a 20-year horizon (and around 30 times over 100 years).
Biological source: microbial fermentation in ruminant digestive systems.
Economic implication: large, immediate climate value per unit reduction.
Social value range: $22 to $38 per animal-year, with $30 used as a central estimate.
Interpretation: this is not just an emissions problem; it is a high-leverage opportunity where modest reductions can produce rapid climate impact.

The constraint is economic, not scientific

Even if a methane-reducing intervention is scientifically feasible, it will not emerge at scale without aligned incentives. Farmers do not capture the climate benefit directly, and firms face uncertain demand for a product with no established market.

CHART FOCUS Causal flow from externality and demand uncertainty to underinvestment.

Externality: methane damages are not priced into normal agricultural markets.
Firm constraint: high R&D cost plus uncertain adoption and weak demand signals.
Coordination failure: firms do not build without demand, while buyers do not commit without proof.
Result: underinvestment in otherwise high-value technologies.
Interpretation: the bottleneck is not only science; it is whether the market rewards success.

An AMC creates demand for a solution that does not yet exist

An Advance Market Commitment addresses the coordination problem by guaranteeing future demand conditional on performance. It is not a research subsidy; it is a commitment to pay for verified outcomes once a product is deployed.

CHART FOCUS Required firm-level success probability across scientific feasibility (eta).

Target success probability: theta = 2/3.
Potential entrants: k = 15 firms.
Mechanism: payment is tied to delivered and verified intervention outcomes.
Effect: uncertain demand is converted into a credible future market.
Interpretation: scientific feasibility and market design interact. If feasibility is lower, firms must deliver higher success probabilities to meet the same target.
Chapter 2: Economics and Sensitivities

The baseline case appears highly attractive

Under the baseline assumptions, the AMC produces large modeled benefits relative to cost. Reconstructing the model reveals the scale and structure of these results.

Baseline AMC: $534.2M.
Baseline BCR: 14.41.
Baseline NPV: $10.95B.
Derived values: Benefits = NPV + AMC = $11.4842B.
Derived values: Total social cost = Benefits / BCR ≈ $0.797B.
Derived values: Non-AMC cost ≈ $0.2628B.
Interpretation: the model implies large benefits relative to total cost, but this depends on core assumptions.

Operational design drives cost and dosing frequency

The frequency of administration directly affects both AMC funding requirements and total social cost. This is a biological parameter with direct economic consequences.

CHART FOCUS Dashboard-style AMC comparison across dosing scenarios, including stress-test labeling.

Baseline scenario: lower cost under infrequent dosing assumptions.
Annual dosing: AMC increases to approximately $832M; BCR falls to about 7.2.
Prime + booster: estimated AMC about $1.07B (linear stress test).
Interpretation: economic viability is sensitive to physical implementation design.

Adoption determines realized value

The model scales benefits proportionally with adoption, making diffusion as important as innovation itself. Even a highly effective intervention generates limited value if adoption is low.

CHART FOCUS Dashboard-style BCR sensitivity to adoption under baseline dosing assumptions.

Baseline adoption: 50%.
Explored range: 10% to 80%.
Scaling relationship: Adjusted Benefits = 11.4842 * (Adoption / 0.50) * (Social Value / 30).
Interpretation: policy success depends not only on invention, but on widespread use in real-world systems.
Chapter 3: Feasibility and Interpretation

Scientific feasibility sets the difficulty of the problem

The AMC targets a fixed probability of success across firms. If the underlying science is less certain, each firm must achieve a higher probability of success to compensate.

CHART FOCUS Dashboard-style required p versus eta curve with theta threshold.

Baseline eta: 0.90.
Formula: p = 1 - (1 - theta / eta)1/k, for eta greater than theta.
Results: eta = 0.90 -> p ≈ 0.086; eta = 0.80 -> p ≈ 0.113; eta = 0.70 -> p ≈ 0.184.
Interpretation: strong financial incentives cannot fully offset low scientific feasibility.

Interpretation and what drives the outcome

AMC performance depends on the interaction of biological feasibility, operational design, and adoption. These jointly determine whether modeled benefits are realized.

CHART FOCUS Three-driver synthesis showing biology, economics, and adoption as co-required conditions.

Biology: determines whether the intervention works and how it must be administered.
Economics: determines how incentives translate into investment.
Adoption: determines how much of potential value is realized.
Interpretation: the AMC is not a guarantee of success; it is a mechanism that amplifies success if these conditions align.
