Chapter 1

Problem urgency and the innovation deadlock

Methane is fast acting, high impact, and highly addressable, but demand uncertainty stalls private investment in solutions.

Why act now

A single cow emits 154 to 264 pounds of methane per year, generating over $100 in annual climate damages. With U.S. cattle producing 6.64 million tons of methane in 2022, that adds up to more than $10 billion in social cost. Methane stays in the atmosphere for roughly 10 years, but its near-term warming effect is far stronger than CO2. Fast livestock methane cuts can deliver climate gains while longer-term decarbonization scales.

The innovation deadlock

The deadlock boils down to two questions no one can answer first. Buyers ask: "How can I buy something that doesn't exist?" Innovators ask: "Why should I build something no one is buying?" Without reliable offtake, innovation stalls and capital arrives too late.

AMC breaks the cycle

An Advance Market Commitment disrupts the loop by inserting a credible demand signal. When buyers commit to purchase future low-methane outcomes, innovators no longer need to wait for a market that does not exist yet. Capital moves earlier, and the deadlock unwinds.

Breaking the deadlock with an AMC

An Advance Market Commitment creates a credible demand signal by committing to buy future low-methane outcomes that meet performance criteria. Critically, sponsors pay nothing if no firm succeeds or producers do not adopt. That zero-downside structure is one of the strongest selling points for funders. The forward signal reduces uncertainty and helps private investment move earlier.

Intervention enters the system

When AMC demand is visible, the loop dynamics change. Investors can underwrite commercialization with clearer downside protection.

Why methane, why now

Methane is responsible for roughly 30% of global warming since pre-industrial times, but it only persists in the atmosphere for about 12 years. That short lifespan means cutting livestock methane delivers measurable temperature benefits within a decade. No other single intervention offers that speed at this scale.

Chapter 2

Commercialization pathways and funding design

AMC support shifts across R and D, scale-up, and competitive markets, while delivery design shapes real-world adoption economics.

Where AMCs help across commercialization

AMCs support R and D when products are unproven, scale-up when demand is thin, and market competition when innovators risk being undercut. The common function is demand de-risking so investment decisions happen earlier.

Technology pathways and delivery economics

The AMC is technology-agnostic by design: vaccines, genetics, feed additives, and slow-release boluses all qualify if they meet performance criteria. Each pathway has different timelines, implementation friction, and cost structures. AMC design has to be flexible because implementation choices are economic levers, not only technical details.

How funding flows in practice

Corporates can pay via offtake premiums, intervention subsidies, or verified carbon payments. Producer compensation and adoption design determine how much modeled value is realized in the field.

Chapter 3

Structure, alignment, and launch readiness

Governance choices set risk allocation, stakeholder alignment determines execution quality, and coalition design decides launch speed.

AMC structure options

Sponsors can fund through three mechanisms: paying per pound of low-methane beef (offtake premium), paying per dose administered (intervention subsidy), or paying per ton of verified CO2e reduced (carbon payment). A traditional buying-club structure provides stronger demand certainty while a hybrid AMC plus investment arm can deploy capital earlier. Governance design determines how risk and upside are allocated.

Stakeholder alignment

Innovators, corporates, philanthropy, and governments each play distinct and complementary roles. AMCs succeed when shared rules, timelines, and incentives are aligned across these actors.

Status, funding target, and how to engage

Coordination is led by the Market Shaping Accelerator, Spark Climate Solutions, Global Methane Hub, and The Nature Conservancy, with design input from Nobel Laureate Michael Kremer, CGD President Rachel Glennerster, and Professor Chris Snyder. The launch window targets COP30, and the current objective is a $750M coalition across food companies, supply chains, and climate-focused capital.

Capstone Project Summary: Methane Vaccine Advance Market Commitment (AMC)

This project explores how an advance market commitment (AMC) could be used to accelerate the development and adoption of a methane-reducing livestock vaccine. The core problem is that methane emissions from cattle represent a significant climate externality, but existing market incentives are insufficient to drive rapid innovation or widespread deployment of mitigation technologies.

The work to date focuses on designing a structured economic and policy mechanism that aligns incentives across key stakeholders: vaccine developers, governments, producers, and funders. The proposed AMC would guarantee a future market for an effective methane-reducing intervention, reducing demand uncertainty and encouraging private-sector R and D investment.

The current prototype is a story map that walks through:

  • The scale and urgency of methane emissions in agriculture.
  • The market failures limiting innovation and adoption.
  • The conceptual design of an AMC tailored to this context.
  • Deployment pathways and adoption constraints in livestock systems.
  • Governance and coalition structures required to launch and sustain the program.

In addition, this prototype includes a sensitivity studio that supports exploration of key assumptions, such as adoption rates, social value of methane reduction, and dosing logistics, to show how design choices affect cost-effectiveness and scalability.

At this stage, the project is in a mid-development phase. The core framework and narrative are established, and the next steps are focused on:

  • Refining the economic assumptions and parameter estimates.
  • Strengthening policy design details and feasibility analysis.
  • Improving clarity and usability of the interactive components.
  • Validating key inputs with literature and stakeholder perspectives.

Overall, the goal is to produce a rigorous, policy-relevant design that demonstrates how AMCs could be applied beyond traditional vaccine markets to address agricultural emissions.
