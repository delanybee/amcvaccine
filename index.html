Situation + Complication

Chapter 1

Methane is the fastest climate lever we have — so why isn't anyone pulling it?

The science is clear. The economics are stuck. And the clock is ticking.

From global emissions to a single intervention

Methane accounts for roughly 30% of global warming since pre-industrial times. About 60% of atmospheric methane comes from human activities, and agriculture produces nearly half of that.

Within agriculture, enteric fermentation from livestock is the dominant source — with cattle responsible for 75% of all enteric methane. This funnel, from global greenhouse gases down to cattle burps, is where the AMC focuses its intervention.

Why methane, why now

A single cow emits 154 to 264 pounds of methane per year, generating over $100 in annual climate damages. With U.S. cattle producing 6.64 million tons of methane in 2022, that adds up to more than $10 billion in social cost.

But here's the opportunity: methane persists in the atmosphere for only about 12 years. That short lifespan means cutting livestock methane delivers measurable temperature benefits within a decade. No other single intervention offers that speed at this scale.

The innovation deadlock

The deadlock boils down to two questions no one can answer first. Buyers ask: "How can I buy something that doesn't exist?" Innovators ask: "Why should I build something no one is buying?"

Without reliable offtake, innovation stalls and capital arrives too late.

What if someone moved first?

The deadlock persists because neither side can afford to go first. Risk is real on both sides — and the market that would justify investment doesn't exist yet. Breaking the loop requires a mechanism that credibly commits to buying the outcome before the product exists.

An AMC breaks the deadlock

An Advance Market Commitment inserts a credible demand signal: sponsors commit to buying future low-methane outcomes that meet performance criteria — but pay nothing if no firm succeeds or producers don't adopt.

That zero-downside structure is one of the strongest selling points for funders. The forward signal reduces uncertainty and helps private investment move earlier — unwinding the deadlock from the demand side.

Investment follows the signal

When AMC demand is visible, the dynamics shift. Investors can underwrite commercialization with clearer downside protection, and innovators can plan for scale instead of hoping for it.

Chapter 2

Three technologies could solve this — each needs a different kind of help

Vaccines, feed additives, and genetics are all in the race. Turning lab results into ranch-scale adoption requires more than good science.

Where AMCs help across commercialization

AMCs support R&D when products are unproven, scale-up when demand is thin, and market competition when innovators risk being undercut.

The common function across all three phases is demand de-risking — so investment decisions happen earlier.

Technology pathways and delivery economics

The AMC is technology-agnostic by design: vaccines, genetics, feed additives, and slow-release boluses all qualify if they meet performance criteria.

Each pathway has different timelines, implementation friction, and cost structures. AMC design has to be flexible because implementation choices are economic levers, not only technical details.

How funding flows in practice

Corporates can pay via offtake premiums, intervention subsidies, or verified carbon payments. Producer compensation and adoption design determine how much modeled value is realized in the field.

Chapter 3

The design is ready — the question is who shows up

Governance, stakeholder alignment, and coalition-building will determine whether this launches at COP30 or stalls.

AMC structure options

Sponsors can fund through three mechanisms: paying per pound of low-methane beef (offtake premium), paying per dose administered (intervention subsidy), or paying per ton of verified CO2e reduced (carbon payment).

A traditional buying-club structure provides stronger demand certainty while a hybrid AMC-plus-investment arm can deploy capital earlier. Governance design determines how risk and upside are allocated.

Stakeholder alignment

Innovators, corporates, philanthropy, and governments each play distinct and complementary roles. AMCs succeed when shared rules, timelines, and incentives are aligned across these actors.

The coalition is forming — and the window is now

Coordination is led by the Market Shaping Accelerator, Spark Climate Solutions, Global Methane Hub, and The Nature Conservancy, with design input from Nobel Laureate Michael Kremer, CGD President Rachel Glennerster, and Professor Chris Snyder.

The launch window targets COP30. The current objective: a $750M coalition across food companies, supply chains, and climate-focused capital. The mechanism is designed. The science is advancing. What's needed now is commitment.

About this story map

Methane Vaccine Advance Market Commitment (AMC)

This project explores how an advance market commitment could accelerate the development and adoption of a methane-reducing livestock vaccine. Methane emissions from cattle represent a massive climate externality, but existing market incentives aren't enough to drive rapid innovation or widespread deployment.

The proposed AMC would guarantee a future market for effective methane-reducing interventions — reducing demand uncertainty and unlocking private-sector R&D investment. This story map walks through:

  • The scale and urgency of methane emissions in agriculture
  • The market failures that limit innovation and adoption
  • The economic design of an AMC tailored to this context
  • Deployment pathways and adoption constraints in livestock systems
  • Governance and coalition structures needed to launch

The sensitivity studio lets you explore key assumptions — adoption rates, social cost, dosing logistics — and see how design choices affect cost-effectiveness in real time.

Status: The core framework and narrative are established. Next steps focus on refining economic parameters, strengthening policy design, and validating inputs with stakeholders.
